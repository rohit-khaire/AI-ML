Exp4)

Min Max Norm:
Maps each value to [0, 1]:
x_norm = (x − x_min) / (x_max − x_min)
Best when you need values in a bounded range (e.g., neural networks, KNN).




Z-Score Standardisation
Centers data to mean=0 and std=1:
x_std = (x − μ) / σ
Best when the algorithm assumes a Gaussian distribution (e.g., linear/logistic regression, SVM, PCA).




Both Min-Max Normalization and Z-Score Standardization are used to solve a common problem: "The Apple vs. Orange Comparison."

In your taxi dataset, you have features with completely different scales. For example, passenger_count is a small number (1 to 6), but trip_distance could be 20+ miles. If you put these directly into a machine learning model, the model might "think" the larger numbers are more important just because they are bigger.

Scaling puts them all on a "level playing field."

1. Min-Max Normalization
In simple terms: It squashes all your data into a fixed range, usually 0 to 1.

Why use it? When you want all your features to have the exact same boundaries. 0 becomes the minimum, 1 becomes the maximum, and everything else sits in between.

Best for: Algorithms that are sensitive to the magnitude of numbers, like Neural Networks or K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN).

The Downside: It is very sensitive to outliers. If you have one trip that costs $1,000 by mistake, it will push all your normal $10 fares down to 0.01, ruining the resolution of your data.

2. Z-Score Standardization
In simple terms: It centers your data around 0 and measures how many "steps" (Standard Deviations) a point is away from the average.

Why use it? It transforms the data so the mean is 0 and the standard deviation is 1. It doesn't have a fixed "end" point like 1.0.

Best for: Algorithms that assume your data is normally distributed, like Linear Regression, Logistic Regression, or Principal Component Analysis (PCA).

The Benefit: It handles outliers much better than Min-Max. An outlier will just have a very high Z-score (like 5.0 or 10.0), but it won't "crush" the rest of the data into a tiny range.

Which one should you use for your Viva?
For your specific project (Taxi Fares), Z-Score Standardization is often better because your data has many outliers (as we saw in your boxplots).

One-line Viva answer:

"We use scaling to ensure that features with larger numerical ranges don't dominate the model's learning process; Min-Max is used to bound data between 0 and 1, while Z-Score is preferred when we need to handle outliers more robustly by centering the data around a mean of zero."





Scaling (Normalization or Standardization) is a linear transformation. It shifts and stretches the data but does not change its underlying distribution. If the data was skewed before, it stays skewed after.

An examiner might look at this and ask: "Why are trip_distance and trip_duration at zero when they were so important in your earlier correlation heatmap?"

The Answer: This is a classic case of Multicollinearity.
Because fare_amount is calculated from distance and time, it is "stealing" all the mathematical importance. The model realizes that once it knows the fare_amount, knowing the trip_distance doesn't provide any new information. To fix this for a final model, you would typically remove the highly correlated fare_amount so the model is forced to learn from the raw trip_distance instead.
